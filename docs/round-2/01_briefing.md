# BMHWA Manifesto Site, Round 2 — Analysis & Decisions

**Companion to:** the Round 2 Plan of Action (`02_plan_of_action.md`)
**Date:** 15 May 2026

This document records the analysis of the Round 2 steering-committee survey and
the five decisions it produced. The plan of action sets out *what* gets built
and *when*; this sets out *why*.

> BMHWA is the **Black Mental Health & Wellbeing Alliance**. An earlier
> auto-generated survey summary mis-expanded it as "Black Maternal Health
> Awareness" — disregard that.

---

## 1. Reading the survey

Six responses, all from steering committee members — Kadra, Nisa Chisipochinyi,
Lynette Charles, Yansie, Ariel Breaux, Ruqia Osman. This matters: it is
**steering-group consensus on what the site should do**, not research into how
the wider community will use it. The people who rated "story/testimony
submission" 5.0 are not the people who would submit testimonies. The survey
tells us direction; it cannot tell us uptake.

Feature ratings (out of 5): story/testimony submission 5.00; pledge collection
4.83; campaign toolkit 4.83; data dashboard 4.00; signatory directory 3.80;
press kit 3.17; members-only area 2.67. The last is last for a clear reason —
the steering group does not need a tool for itself.

## 2. The moderation-load analysis

The question going into the meeting: if the site invites submissions, is
moderating them *one hour a week*, or *wading through a hundred messages a day*?

Features split sharply by the ongoing human work they create:

- **Campaign toolkit** — downloads. Zero moderation.
- **Pledge collection** — a name, an org, a line. Light: spam-check, verify.
- **Story/testimony submission** — every item read, consent-checked,
  safeguarding-checked, publish/hold decided. Heavy, and not automatable.

The highest-rated feature was the only one carrying a heavy, irreducibly human
cost — and one no steering-group survey could size, because uptake depends on
community behaviour, not committee opinion. Worse, personal mental-health
disclosures carry a real safeguarding duty.

That analysis is what drove Decision 1.

## 3. The five decisions

**1. The testimony feature is reframed as an organisational-action campaign.**
Not personal mental-health journeys — instead, accounts of what *organisations*
have done to progress manifesto commitments. A couple of seed examples are
solicited, and the campaign runs in a defined, time-limited window. This removes
the safeguarding duty and the unbounded open-inbox risk entirely, and converts
an un-sizable feature into a curated, schedulable piece of work. It also absorbs
the survey's "signatory case studies" suggestion and the previously-parked
mailing-list survey ("Survey B"), now the campaign's intake channel.

**2. Design is done in-house — no external commission.** No funding is
available, and a pro bono commission carries unpredictable delay. BLKOUT
designs the site using the Windrush typeface (a £15 licence — the plan's only
cash cost) plus researched reference examples. The previously-considered
designer approach is dropped.

**3. The data dashboard moves up to Phase 2.** Built around a monthly refresh,
mostly automated via a data agent and NotebookLM, with each refresh producing a
factoid for the BMHWA newsletter. The newsletter's cadence is deliberate: it is
the forcing function that keeps the dashboard from going stale.

**4. No parallel translated site.** Most users have local translation tools, so
a translated mirror would be largely symbolic. Instead the design carries a
visible, deliberate recognition of the diversity of mother tongues across
BMHWA's communities — acknowledgement of that plurality, rather than a parallel
site in any one language, which would quietly rank the others.

**5. Ariel Breaux is the single point of contact for content sign-off.** New
content sections are approved by Ariel rather than the full steering committee,
keeping the project's gates moving.

## 4. Strategic note — the dashboard is Critical Frequency in practice

Decision 3 is bigger than one site. A monthly-refreshed dashboard that turns
government data into a public, drill-downable, newsletter-feeding artefact is
the operational embodiment of the Critical Frequency reframe: *data invisibility
as a political problem, not a comms challenge.* If the BMHWA dashboard proves
the model, the same monthly pipeline serves BLKOUT's own health dashboard and
Critical Frequency directly. Treat it as a prototype with reuse value, not a
one-off feature.

## 5. What is still open

These are the decisions the plan of action still needs (see its Gates table):

- **Content brief sign-off** — Ariel, once BLKOUT synthesises the brief.
- **Dashboard data sources + monthly-cycle owner** — the one remaining gate
  with real weight; the owner role is light (~1 hr/month) but must be named.
- **Ongoing site editor** — needed for handover.
- **Campaign window dates + editorial lead** — light, schedulable.
- **Events page** — confirm BMHWA has events content to populate it.
- **Windrush licence** — confirm the £15 purchase is made before design begins.

For sequencing, gates, owners and timeline, see the plan of action.
