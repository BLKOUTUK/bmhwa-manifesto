The deeper record · why a manifest

We have been counted before.

A manifest is a ship's count of what it carries. Black people were entered on those records as that cargo — kidnapped from home, chained below deck, worked until they died, then tallied as property and weighed against an insurance claim. The clerk's tidy words were always a cover for terror, forced labour and murder. So this time, we keep the count ourselves — and we name it for what it was.

1781
The Zong

More than 130 enslaved African people were thrown into the sea so a Liverpool syndicate could claim them on insurance. In court, the case was argued over cargo, not murder.

Entered as: a loss · valued at £30 a head

People. The drowned — named where we can, never a number.

1948–73
Windrush

A generation invited to rebuild Britain, recorded on landing cards. The Home Office destroyed the cards — then demanded they prove a belonging it had just erased.

Logged as: arrivals → then erased

Here. Present. Ours — and still owed.

Now
This manifest

23 demands, evidenced and dated, each stamped with how long it has gone unmet. Backed by name. Kept by us.

Recorded as: a count they can't massage or throw overboard

The record they can't destroy twice.

The act of overwriting the ledger’s own words follows M. NourbeSe Philip’s Zong! (2008) — the legal record of the massacre, broken open to give the drowned back their voice.

05
Part 1 · Structural drivers
Aimed at: The Government

The Government must ensure the Windrush compensation scheme factors in culturally sensitive mental health support for victims and their families.

Why. Victims endured forced detention, lost jobs and homes, and separation from family — severe, ongoing trauma the state created and has never repaired.

Still owed

08
Part 1 · Structural drivers
Aimed at: Local public services

Fund culturally appropriate advocacy so people know their rights in the mental health system.

Why. Black people are nearly 4× more likely to be detained — and report worse outcomes in care.

16·17
Part 2 · Ways of working
Aimed at: The next government

Reform the Mental Health Act 1983 and guarantee Advance Choice Documents.

Why. Detention ran 3.6× higher for Black people in 2022/23; Advance Choice Documents give patients a voice.

On the record since 2024 — still owed · 2 yrs

Part 3 · Reimagining support
Aimed at: The Government

Abolish school exclusions; replace them with restorative, trauma-informed responses.

Why. Black Caribbean children are nearly twice as likely to be excluded; exclusion predicts new mental ill-health.
